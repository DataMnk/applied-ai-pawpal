# Senior Pet Care (Dogs and Cats 7+): Daily Veterinary Guide

## What Changes With Age
As dogs and cats age, body systems become less resilient. Common changes include reduced muscle mass, slower metabolism, joint wear (arthritis), dental disease, and weaker hearing or vision. Many seniors also show lower stress tolerance and need more recovery time after activity. Internal changes can be subtle at first: kidney, liver, thyroid, or heart disease may develop gradually. Because signs are often mild early on, routine observation becomes more important than waiting for obvious symptoms.

## Recommended Daily Care Adjustments
- Keep a predictable routine for meals, walks, play, and sleep.  
- Use shorter, more frequent activity sessions instead of one long session.  
- Perform a quick daily check: appetite, thirst, urination, stool quality, mobility, and behavior.  
- Prioritize dental hygiene (veterinary-approved brushing or dental care products).  
- Maintain a lean body condition to reduce strain on joints and organs.

## Mobility Support
Older pets benefit from environmental support at home. Use non-slip rugs on slick floors, ramps for furniture or cars, and low-entry litter boxes for senior cats. Provide orthopedic bedding in warm, draft-free areas. Gentle movement is essential: regular controlled walks for dogs and low-impact play for cats help preserve muscle and joint function. Avoid high-impact jumping, sudden intense exercise, and long stair sessions unless your veterinarian has approved them.

## Diet Changes for Senior Pets
Senior diets should be individualized rather than based on age alone. Many pets need controlled calories with adequate high-quality protein to protect muscle. Some require increased fiber or moisture support (especially cats with low thirst drive). Ask your veterinarian whether a therapeutic diet is needed for kidney, joint, cardiac, or weight concerns. Introduce diet changes gradually over 7-10 days.

## Cognitive Health and Scheduling
Cognitive aging may appear as confusion, altered sleep cycles, nighttime vocalization, or reduced interaction. Support brain health with daily enrichment: scent games, food puzzles, short training refreshers, and calm social engagement. Keep household changes gradual. Schedule wellness exams at least every 6 months, with bloodwork and urine checks as recommended. Early detection is the most effective way to maintain comfort and quality of life in senior pets.
